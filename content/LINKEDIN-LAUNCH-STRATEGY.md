## LinkedIn Post Option 1: Story-Driven (Why I Built It)

```
I read 40+ books last year.

I can name most of them. I can tell you the general topic. But if you asked me what I actually thought about any of them — what I agreed with, what I challenged, what changed my perspective — I'd have nothing.

That bothered me enough to look into why.

Turns out, Hermann Ebbinghaus figured this out in 1885. His "Forgetting Curve" shows we lose roughly 70% of new information within 24 hours. Not because we're careless. Because our brains discard anything that doesn't get actively processed.

Reading is passive. Your eyes move across words. Your brain constructs meaning in real time. Then — nothing. No processing step. The information enters working memory and exits within hours.

The fix is surprisingly simple. Two psychological concepts:

1. Active Reading — instead of passively absorbing, you pause and write what YOU think about what you just consumed. Not a summary. Your perspective. This forces retrieval, opinion formation, and connection-making — the exact operations that strengthen memory.

2. Spaced Repetition — your past reflections resurface at intervals. Each re-encounter deepens understanding and reveals connections you missed the first time.

So I built Distill.

It does one thing: after you read, watch, or listen to something, you write a short reflection. Your reflections resurface later. That's the entire product.

No AI summaries. No second brain complexity. No folders or tags. You have to do the thinking yourself — because that's the whole point. The processing IS the learning.

Free to use. Launching on Product Hunt this Tuesday.

distillwise.com

If you've ever finished a book and immediately forgotten what you thought about it — this exists for you.
```

## LinkedIn Post Option 2: Data-Driven (The Psychology of Forgetting)

```
You will forget 70% of this post within 24 hours.

Not because you don't care. Because of a mechanism Hermann Ebbinghaus documented in 1885 called the Forgetting Curve. Without active processing, your brain treats new information as disposable. It enters working memory, briefly exists, and disappears.

This applies to everything you consume. Books. Articles. Podcasts. Videos. Courses. All of it.

Here's what the research shows:

— Highlighting produces almost zero improvement in long-term retention compared to reading alone (Dunlosky et al., 2013). It feels active. It isn't.

— Writing a response to what you read — even one paragraph — forces retrieval, opinion formation, and connection-making. These are the three operations that move information from short-term to long-term memory (Craik & Lockhart, 1972).

— Spaced review at increasing intervals strengthens memory traces exponentially. But for readers, the thing to resurface isn't flashcards — it's your own thinking. Revisiting what YOU thought three weeks ago compounds understanding in a way that re-reading the source material doesn't.

Most people skip the processing step entirely. They finish an article and open the next tab. They close a book and pick up their phone. The window where ideas are still in working memory — roughly 5-30 minutes — closes unused.

I built Distill to close that window.

The loop: start a session, consume something, write what you thought, and your reflections resurface at intervals. No AI. No summaries. You do the thinking because that's what produces the retention.

Free: 3 deep sessions per month.
Pro: unlimited + spaced resurfacing.

Launching on Product Hunt this Tuesday.

distillwise.com
```

## LinkedIn-Specific Tactics

**Profile optimization (do before Tuesday):**

- Headline: "Building Distill — a thinking tool for readers" (or similar)
- Featured section: pin the launch post
- About section: add one line about Distill with link

**Engagement rules:**

- Reply to every comment within 2 hours (LinkedIn algorithm heavily weights early engagement)
- Reply with substance, not "thanks for sharing." Ask a follow-up question or share a related insight
- Do NOT ask people to "like and share." LinkedIn penalizes this
- Do NOT use hashtags excessively. 0-3 max. #activelearning #reading if any

**Formatting rules:**

- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences max per line)
- Use line breaks aggressively — LinkedIn compresses dense text
- First line is everything — it's the hook before "see more"
- No emojis in the first 3 lines (come across as gimmicky for this audience)
- Arrow symbols (→) work well for lists instead of bullet points

**Timing:**

- Post between 7-9am on weekdays (peak LinkedIn feed time)
- Tuesday and Thursday are highest-engagement days
- Avoid weekends — LinkedIn engagement drops 60%+

**Who to tag (only if you have a genuine relationship):**

- People who've publicly discussed reading retention, information overload, or deep work
- Do NOT cold-tag influencers — it looks desperate and they won't engage
