"What did you think of the book?"

It is the most common reflection prompt in existence. It is also the least useful. The question is so broad that your brain defaults to a feeling — "it was good" or "I liked it" — rather than doing the actual work of processing what you read.

Good reflection prompts are specific enough to force a real answer but open enough to let your perspective emerge. They target different layers of thinking: comprehension, personal connection, critical analysis, application, and perspective shift.

Most prompt lists recycle the same surface-level questions. These 30 go deeper. Use them after finishing a book, article, podcast, or video. Pick 2-3 that feel relevant. You do not need to answer all thirty — the point is to find the question that unlocks your actual thinking about what you just consumed.

## comprehension: did you actually understand it?

Before you can form an opinion, you need to confirm you understood the core argument. These prompts test whether you absorbed the substance or just the surface.

1. What is the single most important argument the author is making? State it in one sentence without using the book's title.
2. What evidence does the author use to support their main claim? Is it research, anecdote, logic, or something else?
3. What would the author say to someone who has never heard of this topic? What is the simplest version of their message?
4. Which chapter or section felt the most important to the overall argument? Why that one and not another?
5. What did the author assume you already knew or believed before reading this?
6. If you had to explain this book to a twelve-year-old, what would you say? What would you leave out?

## personal connection: what does this mean for your life?

Comprehension is the floor, not the ceiling. These prompts move from "what did the author say" to "what does this mean for me." This is where retention happens — when new information connects to existing experience.

7. What moment in your own life does this book remind you of? Why did that connection surface?
8. Which idea confirmed something you already believed? Did the confirmation feel satisfying or too easy?
9. Which idea challenged something you currently believe? How did you react — did you resist it or sit with it?
10. If you had read this book five years ago, would it have landed differently? What has changed about you since then?
11. What is one specific situation in the past month where this book's ideas would have been useful?
12. Does this book describe a problem you actually have? Or a problem you find intellectually interesting but do not personally experience?

## critical thinking: where do you disagree?

Agreement is comfortable. Disagreement is where [critical thinking](/glossary/critical-thinking) sharpens. These prompts push you to evaluate the author's reasoning rather than simply accept it.

13. What is the weakest argument in this book? Where did the logic not hold up for you?
14. What is the author ignoring or leaving out? What perspective is missing from their analysis?
15. If you had to argue against this book's thesis, what would your strongest counterargument be?
16. Does the author overgeneralize from a specific context? Would their ideas work in a different culture, industry, or situation?
17. Where does the author confuse correlation with causation, or anecdote with evidence?
18. What would a smart critic of this book say? What would you say back to that critic?

## application: what will you do differently?

Ideas that stay theoretical fade. Ideas that connect to action persist. These prompts bridge the gap between [understanding and doing](/blog/how-to-remember-what-you-read).

19. What is one thing you will do differently this week because of something you read?
20. What habit, decision, or behavior does this book suggest you should change? Will you actually change it?
21. Who in your life needs to hear the core idea of this book? What would you tell them?
22. What is the smallest possible experiment you could run to test the book's main idea in your own life?
23. If you fully adopted this book's philosophy, what would your daily life look like in six months?
24. What tool, system, or process would you need to build to apply these ideas consistently?

## perspective shift: how did this change how you see things?

The deepest layer of reflection is not about the book at all. It is about how the book changed your lens — the way you see problems, people, or possibilities.

25. What question do you now have that you did not have before reading this?
26. Has this book changed how you think about a topic you thought you understood well? What shifted?
27. What other book, article, or idea does this connect to? What pattern are you starting to see across multiple things you have consumed?
28. If you reread this in a year, what do you think you will notice that you missed this time?
29. What is the most uncomfortable idea in this book? Why does it make you uncomfortable?
30. How would you describe your overall perspective on this topic before reading the book versus after? What is different?

## how to use these prompts

Do not answer all thirty. That is a homework assignment, not a [reflection practice](/blog/reflection-habit).

Instead, scan the list after finishing something. Pick the 2-3 prompts that create the most friction — the ones where your answer is not immediately obvious. Those are the prompts doing real work. If a prompt is easy to answer, it is not pushing your thinking.

Write your answers in your own words. Keep it short — a few sentences per prompt is enough. The goal is not a polished essay. The goal is to capture your perspective while it is fresh, before [the forgetting curve](/glossary/forgetting-curve) erases the nuance.

Then revisit your answers in a few weeks. You will be surprised how much your thinking has shifted — or how much it has not.
